My values

If I have been successful, you will not find any information about me online apart from what I link to on my webpage. To make up for that, for those who want to get to know more about me, I will outline some of my values. If I had to choose three values that define me, it would be positive attitude towards effective altruism, digital rights, and free software.

Effective altruism is a movement that thinks critically about how to do the most good, as in how to maximize your positive impact on the world. Two key organizations are 80 000 hours and Giving What We Can. The latter organization, of which I am a member, aims to create a community of people pledging to give 10% of their income to the most effective organizations in the world. Perhaps their style of thinking, and that of the Effective Altruism community in general, is most clearly explained with a quote from their webpage:

For an example of the difference that effectiveness can make, suppose we want to help people suffering from blindness:

  • In a developed country this would usually involve paying to train a guide dog and its new owner, which costs around $50,000.
  • In 2010, there were about 20 million people, mostly in developing countries, suffering from severe vision impairments caused by cataracts. Many of these people could have their vision restored by a safe eye operation, costing only about $1000, according to GiveWell.org
  • For the same amount of money as training one guide dog to help one person, we could instead cure about 50 cases of severe vision impairment.

This concern for putting the effort of improving the world on the things that matter is one inspiration for my Master's thesis. In it, I use statistical methods to evaluate whether ratifiers of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights behave differently from nonratifiers in times of regime change. I find no support for there being a difference in the human rights trend between them, which may seem pessimistic but if it is really the case, it is better to acknowledge it and find more effective ways to improve the respect for human rights around the world. At the same time, I don't want to exaggerate the scope of my study – it looked at behavior during short periods of time and the results do not rule out an effect of ratification in other circumstances.

Effective Altruism also has one branch that is concerned with existential risk – dangers to humanity as a species, for example extinction by asteroid strikes, extreme forms of global warming and malicious Artificial Intelligence. These risks are of course fatal if they occur but the likelihood that they actually will is often uncertain. Therefore, many Effective Altruists are doing the ground work of just figuring out what the community should focus on. For example, Toby Ord, cofounder of Giving What We Can, recently released a book on the subject, called The Precipice: Existential Risk and the Future of Humanity [*]. It's on my reading list.

Digital rights are those human rights that are relevant for all forms of computer use. These include the right to privacy and freedom of expression as applied in the context of the internet. Digital rights are important because they allows people to realize themselves online in a way that is perhaps not possible otherwise. What I mean by this is perhaps best put by Tijmen Schep, author of socialcooling.com:

If you feel you are being watched, you change your behavior.

Big Data is supercharging this effect.

This could limit your desire to take risks or exercise free speech.

Over the long term these 'chilling effects' could 'cool down' society.

If behaving irregularly has negative consequences for us, we will tend to conform to behavior that is considered normal and become risk-averse. This is sure to stifle innovation. Perhaps your wacky idea may greatly benefit others but you can't know until you have tried. If you are weighing the pros and cons of starting your dream project, the prospect of a potential failure following you for the rest of certainly puts a lot of weight on the con side.

Here, a judgment from the European Court of Justice is relevant. In the "right to be forgotten" case (C-131/12) a man complained that whenever someone Googled his name, an article, with his name in it, about the forced sale of property as a consequence of debt came up. He argued that he should have the right to have the search results limited. The judgment came out mostly in favor of the man. What is interesting here is the background. Regardless of whether the sale of his property was a consequence of his immoral actions, he should have a chance to start over and not have the mistake follow him for the rest of his life.

I wrote my Bachelor's thesis on a related theme, which also, like my Master's thesis, connects to the effectiveness of international treaties. I looked at the Privacy Shield agreement between the EU and the United States which allows American organizations to enter into a program where they promise to respect the digital rights of Europeans as set out in the General Data Protection Regulation and thereby simplifying the transfer of personal data to servers outside the EU. My purpose was mainly to construct a game theoretic model of the situation for my own learning but I found that given some conditions, the agreement should be expected to be respected.

Free software is "free as in freedom, not beer". It is open-source software, but with a philosophy around it of promoting sharing and modification.[*] As such, the development is generally done publicly with the source code continuously available, rather than just published once completed. It is in my mind also closely tied to digital rights because when you can see what a program does before you run it on your computer you can make sure that you are the one in control. Of course, nobody has the time to read the code of all the software they are using but when the code is public, it is enough for one whistle-blower to report malicious code for everyone to be warned.
